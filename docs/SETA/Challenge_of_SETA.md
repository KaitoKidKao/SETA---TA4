# SETA International Agentic AI Hackathon 2026

## Challenge Brief for Participants

## 1. Problem Context

**SETA International** is an AI product and service company delivering technology solutions to
clients worldwide. As enterprises grow, business teams often face repeated manual tasks,
fragmented information, time-consuming follow-ups, and operational workflows that require
better automation and decision support.

In SETA International Agentic AI Hackathon 2026, participants will build a Proof of Concept
(POC) for an Agentic AI solution that addresses a real-world enterprise workflow scenario inspired
by SETA International’s business operations. The three domains in scope are

- Talent Acquisition,
- Project Management (PMO)
- and Employee Management.

## 2. Problem Statement

Within **3 weeks** , each team is required to design and build **a POC for an AI** agent that solves
specific scenarios inspired by SETA International’s Day to day business operations.

The final product should clearly demonstrate:

- A well-defined internal business problem.
- A clear target user group.
- A reliable and structured agent workflow.
- The ability to handle multi-step tasks is important.
- The use of relevant data, tools, APIs, or system actions is important.
- A working demo that shows the agent in action.
- Clear value for internal users, such as saving time, reducing manual work, improving
    accuracy, or supporting better decision-making.


The expected outcome is not a simple chatbot. Teams should build an **AI Agent** that can
understand user intent, retrieve or process relevant information, call appropriate tools, and return
structured, useful results.

A key part of this is how the agent is designed, not just what it does. Teams are expected to define
their own agent contract, which means deciding what intents the agent handles, how each tool is
described, and what good interactions look like. This design work is part of what will be evaluated,
so it's worth thinking carefully from the start.

## 3. Use Cases

Each team will be assigned one use case from the following business areas.

### Use Case 1: Talent Acquisition Department

The Talent Acquisition team manages a hiring pipeline that involves a lot of moving parts, and
right now, too much of it is done manually. Key pain points include:

- CV screening: Matching candidates to JDs by hand is slow, inconsistent, and easy to miss
    strong profiles.
- Interview feedback: Chasing interviewers for responses, checking completeness, and
    updating trackers, all manual and often late.
- Recruitment reporting: Pulling pipeline numbers from spreadsheets and emails takes 1– 2
    hours per report with frequent errors.
- Rejection emails: Templates are generic. Sending is often delayed, and personalization at
    scale isn't currently possible.
- Re-engaging past candidates: A large existing database that rarely gets reused because
    there's no easy way to match old candidates to new openings.

### Use Case 2: PMO / Project Management Office

The PMO team needs to monitor project health and manage resources across multiple projects
simultaneously, but the information they need is spread across too many places. Key pain points
include:

- Project health monitoring: There's no early warning system; risks are usually caught after
    they've already become problems.


- Resource and timesheet auditing: Allocated hours rarely match logged hours, and checking
    this manually across projects is slow and error-prone.
- SOP management: Process documents go out of date quickly, and staff rarely know which
    version is current.
- Cross-project resource lookup: No unified way to search across people, skills, project
    history, and availability at the same time.

### Use Case 3: Employee Management Department

The employee management team sits at the center of a lot of sensitive, fast-moving information,
but right now that information lives in too many places and is too hard to access quickly. Key pain
points include:

- Fragmented document storage: Policies and SOPs are distributed via email or buried in
    onboarding folders, with no central place to search, no reliable version control, and no way
    to notify staff when something changes.
- Scattered employee records: Contract details, performance history, training records, and
    disciplinary data all live in separate systems, and HR has to pull them together manually
    every time a complete picture is needed.

## 5. Team Structure

Each team will consist of **3 – 5 members**. Solo participation and teams of fewer than 3 are not
accepted.

Each team must have **one Team Leader**. The Team Leader is responsible for:

- Representing the team when working with the Organizing Committee.
- Coordinating task allocation within the team.
- Managing team progress during the hackathon.
- Ensuring all submissions are complete, on time, and in the required format.
